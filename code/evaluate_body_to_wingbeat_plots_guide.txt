================================================================================
GUIDE: Reading the outputs of evaluate_body_to_wingbeat.py
================================================================================

All files land in <regressor_run_dir>/eval/ when running either:
    python code/evaluate_body_to_wingbeat.py --regressor_dir ... --autoencoder_dir ...
or automatically at the end of body_latent_regressor.py training.

Recommended reading order on a new run:
    1. error_decomposition.png   — is the model good in physical units?
    2. retrieval.png             — does it land on the right wingbeat?
    3. per_dim_r2.png            — which latent dims does the model learn?
    4. duration_scatter.png      — sanity check on the duration head.
    5. reconstruction_examples.png — qualitative gut-check.
    6. rmse_per_channel.png      — per-channel attribution if needed.


--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
1. retrieval.png — "is the regressor pointing at the right wingbeat?"
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Two-panel figure. The whole point: the latent MSE number tells you nothing on
its own, but rank tells you how the prediction places among the population of
real wingbeats.

LEFT PANEL — rank histogram
  For each val example, sort all N val wingbeats by L2 distance from the
  prediction. Rank = position of the true wingbeat in that sorted list.
    rank 0   = the predicted nearest neighbor IS the true wingbeat (perfect).
    rank N-1 = the true wingbeat is the farthest thing from the prediction
               (catastrophic).

  Good:  a sharp spike near 0 that decays fast. Most wingbeats are correctly
         identified or nearly so.
  Bad:   a uniform distribution from 0 to N. Predictions are uncorrelated with
         targets — no better than random.

  Black dashed line: median rank (the headline number).
  Corner box: top-K accuracies. "top1_acc = 0.40" means the prediction's
              nearest neighbor was the true wingbeat 40% of the time.

RIGHT PANEL — percentile histogram
  Same data, normalized to [0, 1] (rank / (N-1)). Easier to compare across
  runs with different val-set sizes.

  Good:  mass concentrated near 0.
  Bad:   flat from 0 to 1.

  Median percentile of 0.5 = random retrieval. Anything close to 0 is great;
  anything > 0.3 is worrying.

2. rmse_per_channel.png — "how wrong is each wing angle, in degrees?"
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Grouped bars: one cluster per L/R wing-angle channel (L_phi, L_theta, L_psi,
R_phi, R_theta, R_psi), with up to three colored bars per cluster:

  "AE floor" (blue)
    RMSE between decode(true_latent) and the actual ground-truth wingbeat.
    This is the autoencoder's own reconstruction error.
    THE REGRESSOR CANNOT BEAT THIS NUMBER. Even with a perfect prediction
    of the latent, you'd only recover the AE's reconstruction.

  "Regressor add" (orange)
    RMSE between decode(pred_latent) and decode(true_latent). The error the
    regressor adds on top of the AE floor, in physical degrees.
    CONDITIONAL ON A PERFECT DECODER.

  "End-to-end" (red)
    RMSE between decode(pred_latent) and the ground-truth wingbeat.
    The actual physical error a downstream consumer sees.

How to read it:
  - "End-to-end" barely taller than "AE floor"  →  the regressor is essentially
    as good as the autoencoder allows. The bottleneck is the AE.
  - "End-to-end" much taller than "AE floor"    →  the regressor has room to
    grow.
  - Channel-by-channel comparison shows whether the error is uniform or
    concentrated in specific angles (phi vs theta vs psi, or L vs R).
    Asymmetries between L and R are interesting — they suggest the model
    handles one wing better than the other, which shouldn't happen with a
    good model since the data is roughly symmetric.
  - Numbers above bars are the raw values in degrees.

If wingbeats_L<L>.npz wasn't found, you'll see only the orange "Regressor
add" bars (AE floor and end-to-end require ground-truth wingbeats).


--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
3. error_decomposition.png — the headline three-bar summary
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Same three numbers as rmse_per_channel.png, but averaged across the 6
channels and shown as a single chart.

This is THE FIGURE to put on a slide. It answers in one glance: "is the
system physically accurate, and is the bottleneck the AE or the regressor?"

  AE floor       (blue):   irreducible AE reconstruction error.
  Regressor add  (orange): what the regressor adds beyond that.
  End-to-end     (red):    what you actually get in practice.

Reading guide:
  All three bars short and close together  →  both components are tight;
                                              the system is good. Done.
  AE floor large, regressor add small      →  bottleneck is the autoencoder;
                                              train a better AE.
  AE floor small, regressor add large      →  bottleneck is the regressor;
                                              tune it or revisit features.
  End-to-end << AE floor + regressor added →  the errors partially cancel
                                              (rare but possible when biases
                                              work against each other).

4. per_dim_r2.png — which latent dimensions the regressor can predict
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

One bar per latent dimension. For each dim k:
    R^2_k = 1 − Var(pred_k − true_k) / Var(true_k)

  R^2 = 1  (near the dotted reference line at top): perfect prediction.
  R^2 = 0  (solid horizontal at zero): no better than always predicting the
           val-set mean of that dimension.
  R^2 < 0  (red bars below zero): WORSE than the trivial predictor. Likely
           overfit on that dim, or that dim is genuinely noise from the body
           kinematics' perspective.

What to look for:
  A few dims at R^2 ≈ 1, others at R^2 ≈ 0 is informative — it tells you the
  latent space has structure the body kinematics can resolve along some axes
  but not others. Combined with the AE's inspect_latent_space.py variance
  analysis, this lets you identify which latent directions correspond to
  body-determined wing motion vs. wing-specific structure that body
  kinematics can't see.

  All dims uniformly low  →  body input is fundamentally not informative
                             enough; collecting more body channels or a
                             different scaling won't help. Different feature
                             representation needed.

  A handful of red bars   →  those dims may be near-zero-variance in val
                             (denominator small, ratio noisy); harmless.
                             Worry only if many dims go negative.

X-axis is just the latent index, no semantic meaning — but if you're
comparing across regressor runs against the same AE, the indices are
consistent.

5. duration_scatter.png — does the regressor get the wingbeat length right?
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

X = true duration in samples, Y = predicted duration in samples. One dot per
val wingbeat. Diagonal y = x reference line in black.

  Good:  dots cluster tightly along the diagonal.
  Bad:   dots scattered widely, or systematically off the diagonal (regressor
         over- or under-predicts duration consistently).

  Common pathology — horizontal band: the regressor is predicting roughly
  the mean duration regardless of input, which means it's barely using the
  body kinematics for duration at all. If you see this AND the duration MAE
  is close to the predict-the-mean baseline from _print_diagnostics, lean
  toward dropping the duration head or increasing its loss weight.

The console also prints MAE in samples and MAPE %, which are the headline
numbers.


--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
6. reconstruction_examples.png — qualitative spot-check
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

A grid of small panels. Rows are random val wingbeats, columns are the three
angles phi / theta / psi. Each panel has six traces:

  Style    Color       What it is
  -----    -----       -----------
  Solid    Blue        Truth, left wing
  Solid    Red         Truth, right wing
  Dashed   Blue/Red    decode(true_latent) — what the AE reconstructs from
                       the actual encoded wingbeat (AE-only reconstruction)
  Dotted   Blue/Red    decode(pred_latent) — what the body→latent regressor
                       + decoder produces from body kinematics alone
                       (end-to-end output)

Reading guide:
  Solid vs dashed gap  = AE error.
                         Small gap means the AE faithfully encodes this
                         wingbeat.
  Dashed vs dotted gap = regressor error.
                         Small gap means the regressor's predicted latent
                         is close to the true latent in decoder output space.
  Solid vs dotted gap  = end-to-end error.
                         This is what downstream sees.

Hunt for patterns:
  - regressor consistently misses a peak amplitude  →  underestimated
                                                       principal component
  - lag or lead in phase  →  duration prediction is off but morphology is OK
  - high-frequency noise cleaned up  →  latent space is smoothing
                                        (usually fine)

Pick --seed carefully. Default is 0 so the figure is reproducible across
runs. Re-running with different seeds is a cheap way to sanity-check that
the "good" examples aren't cherry-picked by chance.


--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
7. evaluation.json — machine-readable sidecar
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

All of the numbers above in one file:

  retrieval              median rank, median percentile, mean rank/percentile,
                         top-K accuracies.
  rmse_deg_per_channel   per-channel arrays for AE floor / regressor added /
                         end-to-end.
  rmse_deg_mean          the same three numbers averaged across channels
                         (these are what end up in error_decomposition.png).
  per_dim_r2             the R^2 array.
  duration               MAE in samples, MAPE %, true min/max range.
  n_val, latent_dim, evaluated_at, plus pointers to the checkpoints and
  dataset used.

Useful for:
  - scripting comparison plots across multiple regressor runs (the same way
    plot_fixed_len_vs_performance.py reads the AE's grid_search_summary.json)
  - paper tables.
